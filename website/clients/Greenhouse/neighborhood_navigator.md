## Data & Methodology

Resonance uses a multifaceted approach to understand a community's nuances and gain deep insight into residents' demographics, psychographics, and behavior. This knowledge forms the foundation of the "Key Persona" and "One."

Primary data sources are ESRI and Kantar. Kantar is a vital part of WPP, the world's leading advertising agency. These organizations gather extensive consumer data through credit card transactions, online behaviors, retail purchases, social media engagements, media consumption patterns, surveys, mobile phone records, and the US Census. Their data scientists construct precise population profiles and accurately predict future behaviors.

Supplementary resources include Gallup, Pew, PPRI, and the Association of Religion Data Archives (ARDA) to create a comprehensive composite of the community.

Resonance's proprietary system — a culmination of tested best practices, decades of hands-on experience, and in-house data analysis — transforms this foundational information into the Key Persona and One.

**Two analytical perspectives are applied:**
- The entire population within 10- and 20-minute drive of the church
- People most likely to expand the church (the "One")

Demographics = statistical data related to population and specific groups within a given area.
Psychographics = the study and classification of people based on their attitudes, aspirations, and other psychological factors.

## Section 6: Your One & Key Persona

### Framework

There are two distinct audience tiers:

**Key Persona** — Broad audience encompassing both genders, with a wider age and income range. The larger strategic target.

**One** — A highly defined individual within the Key Persona. This is the most crucial person. Every ministry decision, event, and design choice should be filtered through the question: *Is this right for my One?* Focusing on this specific individual creates communications, branding, and ministry that will also attract the wider Key Persona and drive broader growth.

## Section 7: Key Persona — Bright Future Families (BFF)

### Overview

These households are clustered in suburban Gainesville and surrounding areas. They are predominantly married couples, often with school-aged children, living in newer or well-established single-family neighborhoods. They are highly educated, high-earning, and digitally connected.

**Household Count:**
- 5,499 within 0–10 minutes
- 11,061 within 10–20 minutes
- **16,560 total within 20 minutes**

---

### Demographics

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|

| Household Income | $110,000–$210,000 |
| Relationship | Married |
| Number of Children | 1–2 (elementary or younger) |
| Education | Bachelor's+ |
| Full-Time Workers | 2 |
| Occupation | Tech, Healthcare, Education, Management, Engineering, Finance |
| Length of Residency | 5–10 years |
| Race/Ethnicity | ~60% White, 20% Black, 10% Hispanic, 10% Asian/Other |

**Education and Income:** Among the area's most educated and financially secure households. Roughly half to three-quarters hold a bachelor's or graduate degree. Household incomes typically span $110K to just over $200K. Most are dual-income families who see education and financial stability as core investments in their children's future.

**Homeownership:** The majority live in owner-occupied single-family homes — some in long-established suburbs, others in newer build communities on the metro's edge. Many have been in their current homes about 5–15 years, reflecting a mix of deeply rooted residents and newer arrivals. Neighborhoods are safe, comfortable, and appreciating.

---

### Motivations (Five Core Principles)

1. **Enhanced Quality of Life** — They prioritize quality in their lives, including their homes, possessions, and experiences. Their top priority is providing their families with the best living conditions, education, and opportunities.

2. **Community Engagement** — BFF families are committed to building strong, supportive communities. This engagement manifests through involvement in local events, neighborhood improvement projects, and charitable activities.

3. **Family Well-Being** — The well-being of their family members, especially their children, is paramount. BFF families work hard to ensure their kids receive quality education, extracurricular activities, and a nurturing environment.

4. **Achievement and Success** — Success is a motivating factor. They set high standards for themselves and their children and are driven to achieve their goals — career success, educational accomplishments, and other personal achievements.

5. **Active Lifestyle and Travel** — BFF families prioritize physical fitness and engage in sports. Travel is another strong motivator; they see it as an opportunity to create lasting memories, explore the world, and enrich their lives with diverse experiences.

---

### Lifestyle

**Activities:** Much of their time revolves around family routines — school drop-offs, extracurriculars, weekend sports, and socializing with other parents. They value experiences over things and are drawn to family travel, community events, and fitness or wellness routines that can be done together or carved out for personal renewal.

**Technology:** They are decisively mobile-first. Calendars, team chats, school portals, telehealth, and digital payments keep the household moving. Streaming, podcasts, and audiobooks fill commutes. Social use is purposeful — Instagram and Facebook for family and community; LinkedIn for work. At church or school, they expect fast, intuitive digital flows — easy sign-ups, clear info, QR check-ins — without friction or spam.

---

### Faith & Identity

**Religion:** Most were raised Christian and still value faith — especially for their children — but do not regularly attend church. They are open to a church that is relational, emotionally safe, and practical: strong kids and students environments, thoughtful teaching tied to real life, and ministries that fit tight schedules. They disengage when church feels politicized, performative, or guilt-driven.

**Self-Identity:** They see themselves as competent, responsible parents and good neighbors — aspirational yet grounded. They prize authenticity over flash, excellence over spectacle, and community over cliques. Above all, they want to be the kind of family that shows up for each other and for their community.

---

### Future Goals

Raise resilient, kind kids. Keep the marriage strong. Maintain financial momentum without sacrificing peace at home. They want a trusted village around their family and a rhythm that leaves room for rest, fitness, and personal growth. Long term: stable equity, healthy relationships, and kids who are confident, grounded, and capable.

---

### Communication

**They are moved by:**
- Real stories from people like them
- Messages that speak to parenting, marriage, anxiety, and hope
- Churches that feel emotionally safe and relationally consistent

**They will tune out:**
- Religious jargon
- Overproduced "show" experiences
- Messaging that feels condescending, political, or overly institutional

What brings them back isn't tradition — it's relevance. A new kind of experience. A reintroduction to Jesus that meets them in their actual life.

*If you can earn their trust, they won't just come back — they'll bring their kids, their neighbors, and the rhythms of their life with them.*

---

### Brand Preferences

These households admire brands that balance quality, convenience, and authenticity. They are drawn to environments — online and real-life — that reflect their values: stylish, modern, family-centered, emotionally grounded, culturally aware, and forward-looking. Church spaces, websites, and marketing that mirror this clean yet inviting aesthetic are more likely to earn their trust and interest.

Technology brands like Apple and Amazon resonate because they simplify complex lives. Apple offers sleek, reliable tools for work, school, and family organization, while Amazon provides frictionless access to everything from household basics to last-minute sports gear. Both represent efficiency amidst busy schedules.

---

### Brand Engagement Strategies

- **Emphasize Family Connection and Belonging:** Create messaging centered around shared experiences, community support, and activities that strengthen family bonds — both at home and in church.
- **Promote Stability with a Fresh Perspective:** Position Greenhouse as a trusted foundation offering spiritual consistency, while also presenting it as modern, relevant, and responsive to today's needs.
- **Highlight Practical Benefits and Real-Life Impact:** Speak directly to the challenges families face — stress, time, finances — and show how faith, community, and programming offer meaningful, tangible support.
- **Deliver Quality Through Simplicity and Clarity:** Keep communication clean, visually appealing, and easy to navigate. Prioritize authenticity, warmth, and value over hype or heavy emotion.
- **Align with Aspirational Lifestyles:** Reflect their aspirations for growth, balance, and better living — through curated events, parenting resources, and enrichment opportunities that feel aligned with who they are and who they want to become.

### Her Story

Grace is 39, married, with two kids (ages 6 and 10). She and her husband both work — she as a healthcare project manager, he in education. They own a home in a suburban Gainesville neighborhood, with a mortgage that reflects their comfortable but demanding lifestyle.

She manages the family schedule, from school drop-offs to soccer practice, while balancing her own career and household responsibilities. Financially, they're stable — household income around $140K — but they feel the weight of busy lives and high expectations.

She believes in God and in the Bible's messages, but is unsure about the inerrancy or historical accuracy of scripture. She is skeptical about hell but believes there is a heaven.

---

### Her Story — Continued

**Quality Education:** Education is a top priority for Grace. She ensures her children receive a high-quality education and carefully chooses their school or educational environment. She actively seeks educational opportunities that will help her children excel academically.

**Safe and Family-Friendly Environment:** Grace is committed to creating a safe, family-friendly environment for her loved ones. She has chosen to live in a community she perceives as safe, with low crime rates and a welcoming atmosphere for raising children.

**Community Involvement:** Grace is an active member of her local community. She participates in neighborhood associations, engages in community events, and collaborates with other parents to foster a sense of belonging. Her motivation stems from a desire to create a closely-knit community where her family can thrive.

**Family Role:** Grace juggles multiple full-time roles — mom, house manager, and professional. She runs the family schedule, packs lunches, and oversees the kids' education, all while advancing her career.

**Family Time and Work-Life Balance:** She is a hardworking mother who is often overwhelmed by her responsibilities, leaving her feeling drained, inadequate, and isolated. While her marriage is stable, she and her husband are beginning to grow apart due to a lack of quality time and financial strain. Her lifestyle leaves little time for personal care (health, spirituality, rest). As a result, friendships suffer, and many longtime friends drift away. Loneliness is now a compounding factor in her already high state of anxiety.

**Faith:** From time to time, Grace considers trying church again, but she knows it would have to be very different from her past experiences. Her childhood church wasn't harmful, but it felt dull and disconnected from real life. She still sees faith as important — especially for her kids — yet she isn't convinced church is essential.

**Roadblocks:** Politics, her spouse, and her children. Grace doesn't want to be associated with the hard-right Christians she sees on TV or social media, and she resists the judgment she perceives from parts of church culture. At home, her husband shows little interest in attending, and she feels awkward bringing it up. Her kids, now old enough to voice opinions, are unlikely to be eager about going.

**Spiritual Growth (Current):** For now, her spiritual and personal growth comes through voices outside the church: authors like Brené Brown, Elizabeth Gilbert, and Glennon Doyle, as well as thought leaders such as Simon Sinek, Adam Grant, and Tim Ferriss. These figures provide a framework of wisdom, emotional health, and purpose that feels more relevant to her daily life than what she remembers from church.

### Her Fears

Grace struggles with feelings of inadequacy and self-doubt. As a parent, she constantly questions whether she is doing enough for her children and fears being unable to meet her expectations. As a spouse, she experiences doubts about her ability to maintain a fulfilling and supportive relationship, especially during challenging times such as when either she or her husband faces job loss.

The fear of impostor syndrome haunts Grace — she may one day be exposed as inadequate in her personal and professional life. Financial problems add to her stress, as she desires more while worrying about not having as much as others.

Existential fears also weigh heavily: cultural changes, natural disasters, and political unrest disrupt her sense of stability and security. These fears compound her feelings of inadequacy, making her daily life a complex interplay of uncertainties and self-doubt.

---

### Her Pains

- Finding enough time to manage daily responsibilities and activities
- Financial constraints that leave her feeling insufficient
- Uncertainty about her parenting skills
- Feeling adrift due to a lack of control over her life; yearning for a sense of direction and agency
- Longing to be part of a genuine and supportive community; grappling with isolation
- Feeling frequently lonely; intensifying need for meaningful connections
- An inner struggle to reconcile personal beliefs with the teachings she received growing up

### Messaging — Most Likely to Respond To

**Parenting & Family**
- *Advantage and Values:* Highlight how Greenhouse provides children with advantages and instills essential values, preparing them for a bright future.
- *Child Safety:* Assure parents that you prioritize their child's safety and well-being, and explain the enriching activities children engage in.
- *Enjoyable Experience:* Emphasize the fun and enjoyable time children will have at the church, ensuring they look forward to attending.
- *Nurturing Spiritual Life:* Gently remind parents that nurturing their child's spiritual life now can lead to a lifelong relationship with faith.
- *FOMO:* She is competitive and doesn't want her kids to miss out on anything. Highlight fun and exciting family and children events and experiences.
- *Harmonious Family Life:* Showcase how Greenhouse brings families together, providing time for family bonding, a supportive community, financial peace, and support for loving marriages.

**Personal**
- *Community Emphasis:* Emphasize the church's strong sense of community, where Grace can find her purpose and personal significance.
- *Hope, Peace, Joy, and Self-Worth:* Convey messages of hope, peace, and joy, reassuring Grace that she is "enough" and valued as a member of your welcoming community.
- *Inclusivity:* Highlight that everyone is welcome judgment-free and that we are here to support one another in finding a better way to navigate life's challenges.

**Anxiety**
- *Rest and Balance:* Encourage Grace to find rest and balance in your community, providing a sanctuary from the stresses and demands of life.
- *Emotional Healing:* Offer support and resources for emotional healing, emphasizing that Grace is not alone in her journey.

**Community**
- *Loneliness and Relationship Healing:* Address the challenges of loneliness and provide a path to healing relationship wounds through the warmth of community.
- *Friendship:* Highlight the opportunity to build meaningful friendships within the Greenhouse community.

**Religion/Spirituality**
- *Open Dialogue:* Promote transparency and honest discussion of faith, assuring Grace that Greenhouse is a safe place to ask questions and explore her beliefs.
- *Faith Exploration:* Communicate that people at Greenhouse are working out their faith together and that one doesn't need to have everything figured out to be part of the community.
- *Inclusivity of Faith Backgrounds:* Emphasize that everyone, regardless of their faith background, is welcome to seek meaning, purpose, and a more fulfilling life within your community.
- *Different Approach:* Convey that Greenhouse offers a different and more positive experience than past religious experiences.

---

### Messaging — Least Likely to Respond To

When communicating with Grace, be mindful of the following:

- Avoid making her feel she needs to do more or is not doing enough. Such messaging creates stress and makes her feel inadequate.
- Avoid messaging that questions her parenting abilities.
- Avoid negative or judgmental tones — they can be off-putting to Grace. Do not vilify or talk down to others.
- Messages that feel bland or similar to what Grace grew up with will not resonate. Instead, focus on positive outcomes and the personal significance of faith in everyday language.
- Avoid clichés or messages that assume she shares the same beliefs.
- Do not overload Grace with more responsibilities. She already feels overwhelmed; messages requiring her to take on more will not be well-received.

## Section 9: Secondary Personas

### College Community

**Population: 54,131**

Students arrive on campus balancing academics, work, friendships, and questions about their future. They aren't necessarily anti-church, but they are skeptical of anything that feels insincere, over-structured, or unimportant. They're not looking for another lecture or obligation — they already have plenty of those.

What they really want is belonging and purpose. They seek authentic friendships, a place to be truly known, and experiences that bring joy and excitement into their college life. They're open to spiritual conversations, but only when those dialogues feel genuine, not pressured.

The strongest connections form before commitment. Many students won't attend a church service alone, but they will come to a cookout, a late-night worship night, a service project in the city, or a rec-sports tournament. These shared moments create a safe space where trust and relationships can grow.

Over time, that sense of community opens the door to deeper discipleship and faith exploration. When planning for the college community, focus on what helps them connect: social events, service opportunities, creative worship environments, and spaces to ask questions and wrestle with faith. For this group, purpose, friendship, and adventure serve as the bridges to discovering Jesus.

### Gainesville Greg

**Population: 16,560**
